The owner is usually the bottleneck. Is it you?

By James Robertson

You've got $10,000 and a dream. You start a business and find early success. You're great at what you do, you build a good reputation, and the business rolls in. Then you wake up one day with an office, seven employees, fifty customers, and things are falling apart.

Your customers are unhappy. Your employees are unhappy. You can't keep track of everything. You have no processes, no structure, and cash flies in and out of your bank account like food in your golden retriever's breakfast bowl. You're working crazy hours, you can't find time to grow the business, you can't fix the problems you know about, and you're terrified of the ones you don't. Gut feel isn't working anymore.

This is a shift a lot of owners struggle to make. You're great at doing the work, but at this point that's no longer enough.

You have to learn to stop working in your business and start working on it. It's a hard leap to make. Maybe harder than starting the business in the first place.

Your job now is to pull yourself away from the day to day and focus on the bigger picture. Build structure. Make decisions on data and facts, not entirely on gut feel, though there's always some value in good instincts. Learn to hire, train, and rely on other people. People who might do things a little differently than you did, and maybe even better.

You slowly take hats off yourself and put them on someone else. Then one day you wake up with a business of thirty-five employees and two hundred customers, and all the hard work is paying off.

If you're in that phase right now, that's exactly the work I do with owners. It's not comfortable, but it's how the business stops depending on you for everything.
