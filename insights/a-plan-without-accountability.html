A plan without accountability isn't a plan. It's hope.

By James Robertson

It's halfway through March, which means if you're a business owner, you're probably already behind on the plans and goals you set for the year. Did I get that right?

How did I know? Because most owners confuse planning with execution. You spent an energized day in January writing down a list of goals, then headed back to the daily grind. The list was in a drawer somewhere by February, and by March it's a distant memory.

Here are three reasons these plans fail.

They're built in isolation

I get it, you're used to going it alone. But when you bring a few key employees into the planning, you raise everyone's visibility of where the company is headed, and you get real buy-in from the people who helped build it.

There's no written plan

You wrote down a list of goals, but there's no context behind them. A written, detailed plan is crucial. It keeps everyone on the same page, and it keeps everyone accountable.

There's no accountability

A plan without timelines, assigned actions, and regular check-ins isn't a plan. It's hope.

You need a structure that keeps the plan front and centre, so it doesn't get lost in the daily grind. That's the difference between a list of good intentions and a business that actually executes.

Building that structure, and keeping it running, is a lot of what I do with owners. If your January goals have already gone quiet, it's worth a conversation.
