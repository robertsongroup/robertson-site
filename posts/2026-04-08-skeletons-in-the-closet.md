IT has a somewhat
unique problem for business-owners.  I
say somewhat because it's an issue that can happen in any role, but because IT
can be a bit of a black box to people that aren't experienced with it, it can
be even worse.

 

The problem I'm
talking about is the skeletons in the closet. 
You've just replaced your IT leader, consultant or MSP.  Within a couple of days the replacement is in
your office painting a bleak picture of a bunch of issues you didn't even know
were there.  There are different ways
I've seen this manifest itself over my career but the root cause is the same -
many business owners just don't understand IT - that's why you hire someone to
look after it for you, just like you do many other functions in your business.

 

Here are the three
main culprits to look out for:

 

**The Charlatan:** 
Small businesses fall victim to this one a lot.  You had someone who loved certain
technologies, and was very, very good at convincing you everything they wanted
was necessary.  You're spending
mind-blowing amounts of money but everything runs well so you didn’t feel like
it was wasted.  They always got multiple
quotes, seemed to find good pricing on the stuff they wanted so it looked like
they were working hard to save you money. 


 

This is the
best-case scenario.  The good news is,
you actually aren't at risk.  Everything
is amazing.  The bad news is that it's
amazing because you've got enterprise infrastructure (with enterprise costs)
running a small business.  It's not just
that you already spent the money - all of that hardware, software and services
have ongoing costs for support, maintenance, upgrades, things you have to
maintain.  They can right-size you over
time, but it IS going to take time and you have to bear those costs in the
interim.  

 

**The Miser:**  You
loved them because they seemed to get everything done so cheap.  You have a lot of issues but they seem to be
on top of it and the low costs are attractive enough that you deal with the
problems.  But then they're gone, and
slowly things start to fall apart because everything was cheap for a
reason.  All your software is open-source
(or worse, unlicensed), your hardware was second hand, everything runs on some
ancient version of Linux and is all being held together by some complex web of
custom scripts and alerts that only works because of the mastermind behind it
pulling the levers and turning the dials on a daily basis.  

 

The new problem is
you have been drastically under-spending on your technology and if you want to
course-correct, you have a long expensive road ahead of you.  It's also very difficult to even figure out
what's there in the first place, so you don't even really know what you're
facing.  You are definitely at risk of
loss here.

 

**The Hack**: 
The worst of the bunch that can even masquerade as the other two.  This one is just, frankly, unprofessional and
terrible at what they do.  Your spending
seems to be in line but things always feel a bit unstable.  They seems to have a handle on it though and
they've been leading the team for 20 years, so you trust them - you just assume
that's what it's always like. 
Unfortunately, he doesn't have a handle on it, and you don't realize it
until he's gone.  

 

The problems are
usually consistent.  Security is poor,
maintenance and updates are poor, backups are unreliably completed and have
never been tested, and there are no best practices, documentation or procedures
anywhere in sight.  Things that are not
visible problems unless you know what you're looking for, or something bad
happens and you suffer the consequences.

 

Why does it
happen?  IT has two problems that cause
this.  First, while there is obviously
lots of education and certification options out there, there are no concrete
professional requirements to be in it. 
Anyone that has the know-how can do it. 
That's always been a huge shortcoming to my profession, in my opinion,
and it's a bigger problem than ever. 
Second, IT is a bit of a black box to a lot of people.  The business owner is good at their business
and the work they do, but IT is very often something outside their expertise.  That's why they hired someone for it, after
all.  So when their IT leader or MSP
tells them something, they often don't have any basis to disagree with them,
leaving them essentially with free reign as long as the costs are justified.

 

So how do you
prevent something like this from happening to you?  Much like most people audit their books, you
should have someone auditing your technology each year.  Your IT leader or MSP may not like it, but
it's worth your peace of mind to ensure someone objective can tell you if
you're carrying risks you don't know about. 


 

An audit doesn't
have to be a major initiative unless you have regulatory requirements that need
to be met.  You aren't combing through
every log, window and config file to find every tiny issue.  You just need an objective 3rd party to take
a look at current state of the key parts of your infrastructure and the
practices surrounding it, and produce a report with the findings.  That could be a 1-2 days of work for a small
business, maybe 5-7 days for a larger one, and probably less if you've got the
same one coming in each year and they're familiar with the setup.

one of these personalities - protect yourself and your business.  And if you're wondering who could do that
