---
title: When nothing happens, you were successful.
date: 2026-03-27T13:26:00.000-07:00
---
One of the biggest challenges for IT leaders and their teams is that when they are the most successful, nothing happens. Systems don't go down, people don't have technical issues, your backup doesn't fail when you need it to, you don't get a ransomware attack or a data breach, productivity isn't hampered by some ancient application someone won't let go of.  Everything is fine.  Nothing to see here.

Then, when all that doesn't happen, your boss or client tells you that everything runs smoothly and you can probably cut some of your budget, reduce staff, delay some refreshes or other projects.  If you're an MSP, you might be having the "Just what are we paying you for anyway?" conversation.  

In other words someone comes in and tells you how to do your job when you're already doing it well, because it's difficult to attach success metrics to things that don't happen.  Success leads to cuts, cuts lead to incidents, incidents leads to blame, someone gets fired, and someone new comes in to start the cycle over again.

If you're a leader coming in to a new role, or an MSP coming in to a new client, it's important to get a baseline.  There are a lot of statistics out there you can measure.  MSPs should have the tools to do all that easily.  If you're in a corporate IT role you may not - and if you don't, that should be job 1.  You can't manage and improve what you can't measure.

I'll discuss the merits, ways to measure these and what they can tell you in a future article:

Service efficiency - Here, you're looking for a balance between improving your ratios while maintaining good response times:

* Tickets created per endpoint/user 
* Tickets closed per technician
* Endpoints/users supported per technician
* Support hours used First call resolution rate
* Ticket reopen rate 
* Time to respond/resolve

Reliability - How available and reliable is everything?  This should be measured for global availability as well as for key applications:

* System uptime percentage (planned and unplanned)
* Mean time between failures
* Change success rates (changes that didn't cause incidents)

Security - How good are you at both preventing and reacting to security threats?

* Threats detected and blocked
* Threats missed, and the resulting impact
* Patch currency
* Vulnerability scan results
* Phishing simulation results
* Security incident count and impact

Backup & Recovery - your last line of defence, and a frequently underserviced area:

* Backup success rate
* Restoration success rate 
* RTO/RPO actual vs. targets
* Time to complete a successful restoration test

Business Value - Here's where the rubber meets the road:

* IT cost as a percentage of revenue
* IT cost per user
* Cost of unplanned downtime

All of these have to be measured over a consistent time period, monthly/quarterly/yearly.  But, they are all specific, quantifiable, measurable metrics that clearly demonstrate the value that IT is bringing to their business, and the change in value that you have brought since being there.  Use the values in the first 4 categories as internal metrics and then use the last one to assign a dollar value to all of it - because that's the language the business not only understands, but can't dispute.  Now, when that inevitable conversation comes you will be well prepared to demonstrate your achievements
